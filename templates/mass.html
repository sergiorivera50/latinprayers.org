How to follow

The Tridentine Mass

Sacrosanctum Missae Sacrificium

The Mass is the unbloody re-presentation of the one Sacrifice of Calvary, the highest act of worship the Church can offer to God. Facing the altar with his people, the priest stands in persona Christi and offers to the Father the Body and Blood of His Son under the forms of bread and wine. It is the same Sacrifice, the same Priest, and the same Victim that hung upon the Cross.

What follows is the order of the Mass according to the traditional Roman rite, as set down in the 1962 Missale Romanum of Pope John XXIII, the form confirmed for the universal Church by Summorum Pontificum in 2007 and offered today by the FSSP, the Institute of Christ the King, and traditional communities the world over. Its roots run back through Saint Pius V and the Council of Trent to the Mass of the early Roman Church.

Beneath all its variety the Mass has one fixed shape: the Mass of the Catechumens, in which the Church instructs through prayer, Scripture, and the Creed, and the Mass of the Faithful, in which the Sacrifice itself is offered and received. This page is an early depiction of that order, and it will grow more detailed as each part is set against the Missal and the standard guides.

The Three Forms

The one rite is offered with greater or lesser solemnity. Solemn Mass is the fullest form, and the Low Mass its simplification; the same order of service underlies them all.

  1. Missa lecta

    Low Mass

    A priest with one or two servers. Everything is spoken, without chant or (normally) incense. The Leonine Prayers follow.

  2. Missa cantata

    Sung Mass

    A priest with servers and choir, but no deacon or subdeacon. The Ordinary and Propers are sung, and incense may be used.

  3. Missa solemnis

    Solemn High Mass

    A priest with deacon and subdeacon and the full body of servers. The deacon chants the Gospel, the subdeacon the Epistle, with full incensation and the Kiss of Peace.

Before the principal Sunday Mass

The Asperges

On Sundays the principal Mass is preceded by the sprinkling with holy water, in penitential remembrance of Baptism. Vested in a cope of the day's colour, the celebrant intones the antiphon and sprinkles the altar, then the ministers, and then the people as he passes through the church. The faithful stand. (It is omitted on Palm Sunday, when the blessing of palms takes its place, and at a Pontifical High Mass.)

Throughout the year

Asperges me

Asperges me, Domine, hyssopo, et mundabor: lavabis me, et super nivem dealbabor.

Thou shalt sprinkle me, O Lord, with hyssop, and I shall be cleansed: Thou shalt wash me, and I shall be made whiter than snow.

Psalm 50

In Paschaltide (Easter to Pentecost)

Vidi aquam

Vidi aquam egredientem de templo, a latere dextro, alleluia: et omnes, ad quos pervenit aqua ista, salvi facti sunt, et dicent, alleluia, alleluia.

I saw water flowing from the right side of the temple, alleluia: and all they to whom that water came were saved, and they shall say: alleluia, alleluia.

Ezechiel 47

Each is sung like an Introit: the antiphon, a psalm verse, the Gloria Patri (omitted in Passiontide), and the antiphon repeated, concluding with versicles and a collect. Then the Mass itself begins.

A Note on This Page

What is shown above is the Ordinary of the Mass, the unchanging skeleton common to every day of the year. The Propers that fill it, the Introit, Collect, Epistle, Gradual, Gospel, Offertory, Secret, Communion, and Postcommunion, are appointed by the calendar and are drawn from the day's place in the Missal and the Ordo.

For each step the page now shows the celebrant's position, the parts said silently, the customary posture of the faithful, and how the rite changes with the form of Mass chosen above (Low, Sung, or Solemn). Still to come are the variations of the day and season (when the Gloria and Creed are said, the chants between the lessons, and the liturgical colours) and, as a distinct future addition, the Requiem, the Mass for the Dead, which keeps its own order: black vestments, no Gloria or Creed, the Dies Irae, the dismissal Requiescant in pace, and the Absolution at the catafalque. These will be set in place as the depiction is verified against the 1962 Ritus servandus, the General Rubrics, and Fortescue, O'Connell, and Reid's The Ceremonies of the Roman Rite Described.
